SURDS

Surds are irrational numbers that cannot be expressed as a finite decimal or fraction. They are often expressed using roots, such as square roots, cube roots, and fourth roots. Types of Surds: - _Simple Surds_: surds that involve only one root, such as √2 or ∛3 - _Compound Surds_: surds that involve more than one root, such as √(2+√3) or ∛(4-√5) - _Mixed Surds_: surds that involve a combination of rational and irrational numbers, such as 2+√3 or 3-∛2 Operations on Surds: - _Addition and Subtraction_: surds can be added or subtracted by combining like terms, such as √2 + √2 = 2√2 - _Multiplication and Division_: surds can be multiplied or divided by multiplying or dividing the numbers inside the roots, such as √2 × √3 = √(2×3) = √6 - _Rationalization_: surds can be rationalized by multiplying the numerator and denominator by a conjugate, such as (√2 + √3) / (√2 - √3) Laws of Surds: - _Law of Indices_: a^(m/n) = (a^m)^(1/n) = √(a^m) - _Law of Surds_: √(ab) = √a × √b Examples: - Simplify the expression: √(4+√16) - Rationalize the denominator: (√2 + √3) / (√2 - √3) - Evaluate the expression: (√2 × √3) / (√4 + √9) Tips for Working with Surds: - Simplify surds by combining like terms and using the laws of surds. - Rationalize denominators by multiplying by a conjugate. - Use the laws of indices to simplify expressions involving surds.
